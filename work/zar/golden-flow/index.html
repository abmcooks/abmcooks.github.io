The Problem

18 screens between a user and their first dollar.

ZAR is a digital dollar wallet for emerging markets — Pakistan, Zimbabwe, Argentina — where data costs money and trust is scarce. Our growth mechanic: someone sends you digital cash, you claim it, you’re in. But every one of these steps stood in the way:

The Insight

We were designing like a bank. ZAR isn’t a bank.

Banks verify first, value later. Wallets work the other way around — you put money in first, then decide how seriously to secure it.

What if the first thing a new user sees is money — their money — already in their wallet?

Not a signup form. Not a waitlist. Not a verification flow. Just: here’s your cash. Welcome. Everything else — security, identity, KYC — can come later, when users have a reason to care. When they have something to protect.

Resequencing

What we deferred.

This wasn’t about removing security. It was about resequencing it.

Required Upfront (Before) Deferred to Later (After)
Phone number + OTPOptional — secure when ready
Email + OTPOptional — add when convenient
Password creationEliminated — auth-less architecture
Name entryOptional — auto-generated username
PIN + biometricsPrompted after first value moment
KYC verificationRequired only for cards, bank transfers

Earn the right to ask. Once users have something to protect, security becomes a feature — not a gate.

The Craft

Numbers tell one story. How it feels tells another.

When a user opens a cash link, they don’t see a loading spinner. They see a choreographed entrance — the cash note slams into view with synchronized haptics, the amount fades up, and the claim button snaps to life. The whole sequence takes under a second.

Learnings

Three things this project taught me.

01
Sequence beats simplification.
We didn’t remove security — we resequenced it. The same verification happens. But by flipping the order — value first, identity later — the entire experience transforms. Users who have something to protect are motivated to secure it.
02
Constraints force clarity.
A 1-week sprint sounds reckless. It wasn’t. The deadline forced us to strip the experience to its essence: what is the absolute minimum between “tapping a link” and “seeing money”? Every screen had to justify its existence. Most couldn’t.
03
Copy is UX.
“Yours to sell” versus “You’ve been credited.” Same information, completely different emotional response. The first creates ownership. The second sounds like a bank statement. In fintech, every word is a trust signal.
