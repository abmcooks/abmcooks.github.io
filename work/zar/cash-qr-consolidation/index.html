Cash QR Consolidation

72% of transactions were happening through Cash QR. The order system we’d built? Merchants were walking around it.

Lead Product Designer On-Ground Research Service Design iOS & Android
The Observation

I went to Pakistan. Not to test screens — to watch.

What I found: over 60% of merchants were bypassing our order system entirely. In face-to-face transactions — a customer standing at the counter wanting to buy dollars — merchants ignored the formal merchant flow. Instead, they collected cash manually and used Send or Exchange. Features we’d designed for peer-to-peer transfers, not merchant commerce.

They weren’t confused. They weren’t untrained. They’d figured out that the order system added steps without adding value for in-person transactions. The app had a formal merchant flow with order creation, status tracking, and multi-step confirmation. The street had cash, a QR code, and a handshake.

The merchants were telling us what the product should be. We just hadn’t been listening.

The numbers confirmed what the streets showed.

The Insight

The transaction happens in the real world. The app just confirms it.

ZAR’s merchant transactions are physical-first. A customer walks into a store. Cash changes hands. The app confirms what already happened — it doesn’t intermediate it.

But our product was designed as if the transaction started in the app. That makes sense for remote commerce. It makes zero sense when two people are standing next to each other.

Digital-First (what we built)
1. Create order
2. Set terms
3. Wait for acceptance
4. Confirm payment
5. Track status
6. Complete
Physical-First (what merchants did)
1. Cash changes hands
2. Scan QR
3. Done

The merchants had already solved this. They just used the simplest tool available. Our job was to make their workaround the actual product.

The Redesign

Kill the order flow. Three steps.

For merchant-to-customer transactions, we deprecated the order system entirely. No partial fix. No iteration. Removed. Orders were retained only for merchant-to-trader interactions — bulk wholesale transactions where formal tracking adds legitimate value.

1
Visit store
Customer finds the merchant on the map or walks in.
2
Exchange cash
Physical handoff. The real transaction.
3
Scan QR
The app confirms the exchange. Dollar amount. Rate. Done.

Three steps. No orders. No status tracking. No acceptance flow. The app serves the moment — it doesn’t replace it.

Simplification

8 fields to 2. Discovery only. Present tense.

The merchant map had been trying to do two things: help users find merchants AND initiate transactions. I stripped transaction initiation entirely. The map became discovery-only with clear instructions: “1. Visit store. 2. Exchange cash. 3. Scan QR.”

Merchant Control

The merchant sets the terms.

One principle guided the redesign: the merchant sets the terms. In the old flow, exchange rates were part of the order negotiation. In the new flow, the merchant sets their rate upfront. It’s displayed on their profile. Customers see it before they walk in. If they show up, they’ve already agreed.

Merchants can adjust their rate per transaction — responding to market conditions, competition, or customer relationships. The system enables their business judgment instead of constraining it with a formal order protocol.

Impact

We didn’t change merchant behavior. We changed the product.

Organic adoption 60%+ already using Cash QR Now the only option

The strongest validation: we didn’t change merchant behavior. We changed the product to match the behavior that was already working. 72% of volume was already flowing through Cash QR. We just removed the 28% that was fighting reality.

Learnings

What this taught me.

01
Watch before you design.
60% of merchants had already solved the problem we were trying to solve. They didn’t need a better order flow — they needed us to stop forcing one. The most important research I did wasn’t user testing. It was standing in a store and watching.
02
Remove before you add.
The instinct is always to build: a better order flow, a smarter status system, a more elegant confirmation screen. The right answer was to remove all of it. The feature that best served merchants was the absence of features they didn’t need.
03
Digital should serve physical, not replace it.
In emerging market fintech, the transaction happens in the real world. Cash changes hands. People shake on it. The app’s job is to confirm and record — not to intermediate. Products that try to digitize the human interaction will always lose to the workaround.
04
Adoption data is design research.
72% Cash QR adoption wasn’t a metric — it was a design brief. The users had already voted with their behavior. Listening to adoption patterns is the fastest form of user research, if you’re willing to hear that your product is wrong.
