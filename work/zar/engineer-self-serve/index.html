Eliminating the Design Bottleneck — On Purpose

“What if the best thing a design leader could do was remove design from the critical path?”

Lead Product Designer Strategy + AI + Org Change 6 Weeks ZAR (a16z, Multicoin)
The Problem

2 designers. 12+ engineers. One bottleneck.

2
Designers
12+
Engineers

Every feature required design before engineering could start. Features rotted in a queue called “Needs Design” while engineers context-switched. The roadmap was hostage to my bandwidth.

The Politics

Three bosses. Three different answers.

Everyone agreed design was a bottleneck. Nobody agreed on the fix.

CEO + CTO
Full automationEngineers design with AI. No designers needed.
Head of Engineering
Consultant modelDesigners advise. Engineers own design decisions.
Me
I didn’t know yetBut I knew the answer wasn’t picking a side.

My job was to turn this from a debate into an experiment.

V1 — Weeks 1-2

Structured guidelines for AI

V1 // STRUCTURED GUIDELINES
Built a two-layer system
design-system.md — Brand principles written for an AI audience. Explicit, no assumed context.

Guidelines.md — Component rules, layout patterns, ZAR visual language. Everything an AI tool would need to assemble a screen.

Fed it to Figma Make. It generated screens. They looked like a designer had described ZAR to someone who’d never seen it.
“Inadequate.”
Head of Engineering’s one-word review after the 1:1 demo.

But the failure revealed the real problem: the gap wasn’t in the guidelines — it was between our design system and what the AI could actually access. Words about components aren’t components.

V2 — Weeks 3-4

Two paths. Run in parallel. Data over opinions.

Path A — Automation
Claude Code → Flutter UI

Technically correct but lifeless screens. No soul, no brand feel.
Fast Not scalable alone
Path B — Process
Designer-as-Consultant

Better quality, but still required significant designer time. The bottleneck moved, it didn’t disappear.
Better quality Still depends on me

Neither approach alone worked. But together, they pointed to an answer: the AI needed direct access to the design system — not descriptions of it.

V3 — Week 5

The breakthrough

V3 // DIRECT SYSTEM ACCESS
Connected AI directly to Figma
Gave Claude read/write access to Techtonic, our 670-component design system, via Figma Console MCP.

Described a screen verbally. No mockups. No guidelines. Just words.

Claude created the screen using our actual components. Correct colors. Correct typography. Correct spacing. Not a generic mockup — a screen that belonged in our app.

The AI wasn’t designing. It was assembling — the same way a trained designer works from a system.

The quality of AI output is directly proportional to the quality of your design system. No system, no magic.

Learnings

Four things this project taught me.

01
AI amplifies design systems.
Without Techtonic (670 components), the AI had nothing to work with. The quality of AI-generated design is directly proportional to the quality of your design system. This is the strongest argument for investing in a system early.
02
Fail fast, in public.
V1 failed in front of the Head of Engineering. That was uncomfortable. But showing the failure built trust — it proved I was solving the real problem, not just shipping a demo.
03
Navigate, don’t pick sides.
When leadership disagrees, a designer’s job isn’t to choose — it’s to generate evidence that helps everyone converge. Running parallel experiments turned a political problem into an empirical one.
04
Make yourself unnecessary.
My instinct was to protect design’s role. The breakthrough came when I realized my job wasn’t to stay essential — it was to make design thinking available everywhere, through better systems, better tools, and better access.
