The Constraints

Year One

Four principles that shaped every screen

The engineering team had been working with outsourced design — screens that looked like a Western banking app transplanted to Lahore. Clean, but wrong. I scrapped the approach and started from first principles.

📷
Before / After: Outsourced vs. ABM’s Design
Side-by-side comparison of the outsourced Western-style screens vs. the redesigned approach
The gap between outsourced design and designing for Pakistan.
01
Progressive Disclosure
Don’t show everything at once. A first-time user needs their balance and one button to send money. Not investment options.
02
Generous Feedback
Every tap confirmed. Every transfer acknowledged. In a market where “did it work?” creates anxiety, silence is the enemy.
03
Error Prevention
Don’t tell users they made a mistake. Design flows where mistakes are impossible. Catch it before they hit send.
04
Celebration Moments
Your first digital transfer ever? That deserves more than a green checkmark. We made it feel like an achievement.

The System

By year two, I was designing every screen. Engineers interpreted Figma files differently. Colors drifted. Spacing multiplied. The problem wasn’t the team — it was the absence of a system.

I built SadaDS from scratch. Not a component library. A system — tokens connected directly to production code.

What I Learned

Five things SadaPay taught me

01
Design systems pay exponential dividends
SadaDS felt slow to build. Three months of infrastructure while features waited. But once live, the compounding effect was immediate — every new feature was faster, every handoff was cleaner, every engineer became a design advocate.
02
Service design is product design’s superpower
The biggest impact I had wasn’t on any single screen — it was the fraud blueprint. Mapping the entire journey revealed problems that no amount of UI iteration would have found.
03
Leadership is letting go
My instinct was to review every pixel. The breakthrough came when I stopped directing and started enabling — clear standards, strong systems, and trust.
04
Context is everything
Patterns that work in developed markets fail in emerging ones. “Swipe to confirm” assumes users have swiped before. Designing for Pakistan taught me that good design is never universal. It’s always specific.
05
Physical products carry brand weight
The numberless card taught me something digital products rarely do: a physical artifact can become your most powerful brand signal. People showed their SadaPay cards to friends. It was a conversation starter, a status symbol, and a trust signal.
