The waitlist didn’t just collect signups. It filtered for commitment. The people who earned their way in became the ones who never left.

Same playbook. Evolved. Designed the waitlist as a product — twice.

A waitlist is your first product.

Most companies treat pre-launch as a signup form and a “we’ll let you know” email. The user gives you their attention once — and you give them nothing back.

I designed waitlists as hook systems — layered mechanics that reward specific behaviors, create social proof, and filter for users who actually want to engage. Not email collection. Commitment filtration.

The hook didn’t expire. The identity stuck.

2026 — Three years after I left
“I hold a Founder’s Club card. I invited 10 people to get it.”
People still introduce themselves to me this way. The waitlist mechanic became part of how they relate to the product — permanently.

Founder’s Club members became power users. They ordered cards immediately. They transacted daily. They brought more people in after launch, not just during the waitlist. The gamification didn’t grow a list — it pre-qualified an entire user base.

700,000+ signups. Zero paid acquisition. Pure word-of-mouth. And the users who earned their way in stayed.

ZAR — the evolution

ZAR operates in 100+ countries. Different cultures, different motivations. A single mechanic wouldn’t scale. The system needed four layers of hooks.

One hook works for one market. Layers work globally.

1
Active Waitlist Dashboard
Stories, points capsule, reward tiles, dynamic position-aware CTA. The waitlist feels like an app, not a queue.
2
Multi-Action Points
Invite friends, verify email, follow social, claim username. Not everyone refers 10 friends — but everyone does something.
3
Golden Flow — Invisible Referral
Send money to a non-user. They join. Sender gets credit. No referral link. The product action IS the invitation.
4
Campaign Spikes (PrismFest)
Time-limited scratch tickets. Every 3 referrals = 1 ticket. Campaign urgency on top of structural virality.

Status over access. The black card wasn’t about getting in faster — it was about being different. Points and rewards create visible progress. The reward is who you become, not just what you get.

Effort creates loyalty. The IKEA effect — people value what they build. A user who earned their way through the waitlist values the product more than one handed instant access. The friction is the feature.

Physical anchors. SadaPay’s black card. The merchandise. Physical objects create emotional weight that digital rewards can’t match. You can delete an app. You can’t unknow the card in your wallet.

Four lessons from two waitlists

01
Gamification is filtration.
The users who complete hard tasks — inviting 10 real people — are the users who transact daily after launch. Design the effort, design the user base.
02
Physical rewards create permanent identity.
Digital badges expire emotionally. A black card in your wallet doesn’t. Three years later, it’s still how people introduce themselves.
03
One hook for one market. Layers for the world.
SadaPay’s single mechanic was perfect for Pakistan. ZAR needed four layers for 100+ countries. The principle transfers; the implementation adapts.
04
The best referral is invisible.
Golden Flow: users don’t want to be marketers. If using the product naturally brings new users, the growth loop runs itself.

The waitlist is the product. The effort is the filter. The reward is the identity. Design all three, and you don’t just grow a list — you grow a user base that stays.
