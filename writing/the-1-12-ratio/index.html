The 1:12 ratio — what being the only designer taught me

Lessons from being the first and only designer at two startups, surrounded by engineers who needed design decisions faster than I could make them.

Leadership Startups Design Culture

Twice in my career, I’ve been the first designer at a startup. Not the first senior designer, not the first product designer — the first designer, period. One person responsible for every screen, every icon, every flow, every pixel that would touch users.

At SadaPay, it was 1 designer to 12 engineers building Pakistan’s first neobank for 220 million people. At ZAR, it was founding designer for an a16z-backed Solana wallet with no design system, no established patterns, and a product thesis that needed proving in the streets of Lahore.

Being the only designer in the room teaches you things that managing a team of 8 never will.

You can’t design every screen

This was the hardest lesson. When you’re the only designer and there are 12 engineers shipping features in parallel, you physically cannot design every screen before it gets built. Something has to give.

At first, I tried to keep up. Late nights, weekends, a Figma file that grew like an organism. It lasted about two months before the quality started dropping. Not because I was less capable, but because speed and craft are in tension when there’s no system backing you up.

The fix wasn’t working harder. It was building systems.

I stopped designing screens and started designing decisions. A design system (SadaDS at SadaPay, Techtonic at ZAR) meant that 80% of design decisions were pre-made. Engineers didn’t need to wait for me to decide what a button looks like. The system already knew.

The most productive thing a solo designer can do is make themselves unnecessary for the routine so they can focus on the novel.

Every decision is a bottleneck

When you’re the only designer, you are the bottleneck for every visual decision in the company. Every “should this be a bottom sheet or a full screen?” question routes through you. Every icon request. Every color choice. Every “quick review” that takes 45 minutes.

I had to learn to triage ruthlessly:

  • High-stakes flows (onboarding, KYC, money movement) — I designed these myself, pixel-perfect, no delegation
  • Standard screens (settings, profile, lists) — I created patterns and let engineers assemble from the system
  • Edge cases (error states, empty states, loading) — I documented principles and let the team apply them

This isn’t about lowering standards. It’s about knowing where craft matters most and investing your limited time there.

Engineers become your design team

Here’s the counterintuitive part: when you’re the only designer, engineers don’t just build your designs — they start making design decisions. Whether you want them to or not.

You have two choices: fight this (and lose), or embrace it and give them the tools to make good decisions.

I chose the second. At both SadaPay and ZAR, I invested heavily in documentation that wasn’t for designers — it was for engineers. Not “here’s the design system showcase.” More like: “when a user encounters an error, here are the three patterns you can use, here’s when to use which, here’s the Figma component, here’s the code snippet.”

The result? Engineers started shipping screens I hadn’t designed that looked and felt like I had designed them. That’s not a sign of less design quality. It’s a sign the system is working.

Your taste is your moat

If engineers can assemble 80% of screens from a design system, what’s the designer for?

The 20% that can’t be systematized.

The moment a user sends their first digital transfer and deserves a celebration, not a green checkmark. The physical debit card that has nothing printed on it and becomes a cultural icon. The decision to kill an entire order system because you stood in a merchant’s shop and watched them ignore it.

Systems handle consistency. Taste handles the moments that make people feel something. The 1:12 ratio forces you to understand the difference, because you don’t have time to apply taste to everything.

It changes how you hire

When I finally got to build a team at SadaPay (1 to 8 designers over two years), the 1:12 experience shaped everything about how I hired.

I didn’t want designers who needed to be told what to design. I’d been that person for too long — I knew the company needed people who could operate independently. So I designed the hiring process around autonomy:

  1. Portfolio reviews tested problem-solving, not visual polish. “Walk me through a decision you made that was wrong, and how you knew.”
  2. Design challenges mirrored real constraints. “Design a first-time KYC flow for someone who’s never held a bank account.” Not “redesign Spotify.”
  3. Culture fit meant craft culture. Weekly crits where the newest junior could challenge the lead’s work. If you couldn’t handle that, you wouldn’t thrive here.

The team I built went on to design at Revolut, at top agencies, at their own startups. I’m prouder of that than any screen I shipped.

“He consistently trusted and empowered us to take ownership of our projects, fostering an environment of creativity and innovation.”
— Sohaib Arif, now at Revolut

The ratio gets easier. Then it gets hard again.

At SadaPay, the ratio went from 1:12 to 8:60. At ZAR, I’m scaling again. Each time, the early ratio forces a discipline that pays dividends later:

  • You build systems before you need them
  • You document decisions instead of hoarding them
  • You learn what actually requires design craft vs. what just requires a pattern
  • You stop seeing “I didn’t design that screen” as failure

The 1:12 ratio is brutal. But it teaches you something that a comfortable 1:4 ratio never will: design leadership isn’t about designing more. It’s about designing what matters and enabling everything else.

If you’re the only designer at your company right now: build the system first. The screens can wait. Your future team will thank you.
