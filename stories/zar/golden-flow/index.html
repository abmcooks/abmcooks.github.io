18 STEPS TO 4 — THE GOLDEN FLOW

Redesigning crypto wallet onboarding for emerging markets. A 77.8% step reduction that made the difference between users finishing and users leaving.

Company ZAR Year 2024 Role Head of Design

THE CHALLENGE

ZAR's onboarding flow was 18 steps long. For a crypto wallet targeting emerging markets — users who may be encountering crypto for the first time — that's not an inconvenience, it's a wall. Every additional step is a chance to lose someone who was curious but not yet committed.

The existing flow was designed by engineers who added screens as requirements came in: email verification, phone verification, KYC document upload, selfie capture, address entry, security setup, wallet creation, seed phrase backup, and more. Each step made sense in isolation. Together, they created an experience that felt like filling out a government form.

The data confirmed what the design suggested: completion rates were low, and most drop-offs happened in the middle of the flow where the cognitive load peaked.

77.8% reduction in onboarding steps

THE APPROACH

I mapped every step of the existing flow, then categorized each one: essential (legally required or technically necessary), important (adds value but can be deferred), and removable (legacy, redundant, or premature).

The key insight was progressive disclosure. Not every piece of information needs to be collected before the user gets value. A crypto wallet can be useful with just a verified identity — the rest can come later, when the user is invested in the product and motivated to complete additional steps.

I redesigned the flow around the principle of "get them to value first." The minimum viable onboarding: verify your identity, create your wallet, start exploring. Everything else — additional security, advanced KYC tiers, seed phrase backup — was moved to post-onboarding prompts triggered by user behavior.

Each of the remaining 4 steps was redesigned to feel quick. Progress indicators, pre-filled data where possible, smart defaults, and micro-copy that explained why each step mattered.

THE SOLUTION

The redesigned Golden Flow was 4 steps: identity verification (streamlined with document scanning and liveness check in one screen), wallet creation (automatic, with a clear explanation of what just happened), security setup (biometric lock, presented as a benefit not a chore), and a welcome screen showing the user their empty-but-ready wallet.

Deferred steps were introduced through contextual prompts: when a user first tries to send a large amount, they're prompted for enhanced KYC. When they accumulate a meaningful balance, they're guided through seed phrase backup. The timing matched the user's motivation.

The visual design used ZAR's brand to make each step feel fast and premium — dark backgrounds, clear typography, generous spacing, and animations that moved things forward rather than making users wait.

THE IMPACT

18 → 4 Steps reduced
77.8% Step reduction
Higher Completion rates
Faster Time to value

The Golden Flow transformed ZAR's onboarding from a barrier into a bridge. Completion rates improved significantly, and the deferred-step approach meant users who completed onboarding had a higher engagement rate — they'd already experienced the product's value before being asked to do more.

For a crypto product targeting emerging markets, where every user's first interaction with the technology might happen through your app, removing friction isn't just a UX improvement — it's the difference between adoption and abandonment.
